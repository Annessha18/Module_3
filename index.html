01 — Introduction

The Human Cost of
Road Collisions

Every single day, roads around the world become the setting for thousands of collisions that permanently alter lives. Road traffic accidents are among the leading causes of injury, disability, and death globally, claiming over 1.19 million lives each year according to the World Health Organization. Far from being random events, these collisions follow patterns shaped by infrastructure design, driver behavior, environmental conditions, and policy decisions. Understanding these patterns is the first step toward building safer transportation systems that protect everyone who travels — drivers, passengers, cyclists, and pedestrians alike. The roads we drive on are products of complex engineering and societal choices, and so too are the accidents that occur on them.

In the United Kingdom alone, the road network spans over 400,000 kilometers, carrying tens of millions of vehicle journeys daily. While the UK has made remarkable strides in reducing road fatalities since the 1970s — through seat-belt legislation, speed enforcement, road redesign, and public education campaigns — thousands of serious injuries still occur each year. The collision data collected by police forces across Great Britain provides one of the world's most detailed records of road accidents, capturing not just location and severity but also the surrounding environmental context: road type, weather, lighting, speed limit, and time of day. This depth of data makes it an exceptional resource for empirical research and data-driven policymaking.

Road safety is not purely an engineering problem. It intersects with public health, behavioral psychology, urban planning, and social equity. Lower-income neighborhoods often have higher pedestrian fatality rates due to fewer traffic-calming measures and faster roads. Nighttime driving and driver impairment are disproportionately implicated in fatal crashes even though they represent a minority of total journeys. Young male drivers statistically face elevated crash risks — a pattern observed across cultures and decades. Seasonal factors such as winter darkness and icy road conditions introduce additional layers of complexity. Any serious attempt to reduce road harm must grapple with this multi-dimensional landscape, which is precisely where data science offers a transformative lens.

The consequences of road accidents extend far beyond the immediate physical trauma. Families are fractured by sudden loss. Survivors face long-term physical rehabilitation, psychological trauma including PTSD, and economic hardship from medical bills and lost income. Emergency services are stretched thin by the volume of collision responses. Healthcare systems absorb enormous costs — road crashes are estimated to cost the UK economy over £33 billion annually when accounting for human costs, medical treatment, police response, and property damage. Communities near accident hotspots experience reduced quality of life and lower property values. These ripple effects make road safety a systemic societal issue, not merely a personal one.

The application of machine learning and data science to road safety research represents a transformative opportunity. Modern algorithms can detect non-linear interactions between dozens of variables simultaneously — uncovering how the combination of a wet road surface, a 60mph speed limit, and low-light conditions dramatically compounds accident severity even when each factor alone appears manageable. Clustering algorithms identify geographic and temporal accident hotspots with far more precision than manual mapping. Decision trees generate interpretable rules that transportation engineers and policymakers can act upon directly. This project applies the full data science lifecycle — from raw data cleaning through advanced supervised and unsupervised modeling — to extract actionable intelligence from real 2023 UK collision records, with the ultimate goal of informing safer road environments for everyone.

03 — Dimensionality Reduction

Principal Component
Analysis

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is an unsupervised linear technique that transforms a set of possibly correlated variables into a smaller set of linearly uncorrelated principal components ordered by the amount of variance they capture. Each component is an eigenvector of the data's covariance matrix; the corresponding eigenvalue represents how much variance lies along that direction. PCA is used here for visualization (projecting to 2D and 3D), feature analysis, and as preprocessing before clustering — reducing computation while retaining the majority of information in the dataset.

04 — Unsupervised Learning

Clustering
Analysis

K-Means

A partition-based method that assigns every point to the nearest centroid, iteratively minimizing within-cluster variance. Requires specifying k in advance and assumes roughly spherical clusters. Computationally efficient at large scale. Best for discovering well-separated, globular groups in normalized data.

Hierarchical

Builds a nested tree (dendrogram) of cluster relationships using agglomerative (bottom-up merge) or divisive (top-down split) linkage. Does not require specifying k and is fully interpretable via the dendrogram. Computationally expensive on large datasets but excellent for revealing multi-scale structure.

DBSCAN

A density-based method that groups points with at least min_samples neighbors within radius ε, marking low-density points as noise. Discovers arbitrarily shaped clusters without requiring k. Ideal for geospatial accident hotspot detection where cluster shapes are irregular and outliers exist.

Six features were selected for clustering: longitude, latitude, speed_limit, hour, month, and number_of_vehicles. The label (collision_severity) was removed and stored separately for post-hoc comparison. Data was standardized with StandardScaler and optionally reduced to 3D via PCA (52.1% variance retained) for efficiency.

05 — Association Rule Mining

Association
Rule Mining

Association Rule Mining (ARM) discovers interesting co-occurrence patterns in transactional data. Rules take the form A → B, meaning "when A appears, B also tends to appear." Three measures define rule quality: Support (proportion of transactions containing both A and B), Confidence (given A, how often does B also occur), and Lift (how much more likely B is given A compared to chance — lift >1 means genuine positive association above baseline).

The Apriori Algorithm

Apriori exploits the anti-monotone property: if an itemset is infrequent, all its supersets must also be infrequent. The algorithm generates candidate itemsets level by level (1-item → 2-item → 3-item...), pruning any with support below the minimum threshold. Rules are extracted from all surviving frequent itemsets. Thresholds used here: min_support = 0.05, min_confidence = 0.50.

Each road accident was treated as one transaction. Continuous features were binned: speed limit → low (≤30mph), mid (31–50mph), high (>50mph); hour → rush_hour, night, or day. No severity label was included in the transactions — ARM discovers patterns without knowing collision outcomes.

06 — Supervised Learning

Naïve Bayes
Classification

Naïve Bayes (NB) classifiers are probabilistic models based on Bayes' Theorem that assume conditional independence between features given the class label. This strong assumption is rarely fully true in practice, but still enables competitive performance, particularly on high-dimensional data. NB is fast, interpretable, produces probabilistic outputs, and works well with smaller training sets.

P(class | features) ∝ P(class) × ∏ P(feature_i | class)

07 — Supervised Learning

Decision Tree
Modeling

Decision Trees (DTs) are hierarchical classifiers that recursively partition the feature space into increasingly pure regions. At each internal node, the algorithm selects the feature and split threshold that maximally reduces impurity. At leaf nodes, class labels are assigned by majority vote. DTs are highly interpretable — results read as if-then rules — handle both numerical and categorical features, and require minimal preprocessing. Their main weakness is a tendency to overfit when allowed to grow deep without constraints.

GINI Impurity

Measures the probability of misclassifying a randomly chosen sample. A Gini of 0 means the node is perfectly pure (all one class). sklearn's DecisionTreeClassifier uses Gini as its default criterion.

Gini(S) = 1 − Σ pᵢ²

Worked Example

A node: 70% Slight, 30% Serious.
Gini = 1 − (0.7² + 0.3²) = 1 − (0.49 + 0.09) = 0.42

Entropy & Information Gain

Entropy measures disorder of a node. Information Gain (IG) is the reduction in entropy after a split — the algorithm chooses the split with maximum IG at each node.

H(S) = −Σ pᵢ log₂(pᵢ)
IG = H(parent) − Σ (|Sᵥ|/|S|) · H(Sᵥ)

Worked Example

Parent entropy H = 1.35 bits. Split on speed_limit → child H̄ = 0.97 bits.
IG = 1.35 − 0.97 = 0.38 bits

Why Infinite Trees Are Possible

A decision tree can grow until every leaf contains exactly one training sample, perfectly memorizing the data. For any continuous feature, there are infinitely many possible split thresholds (speed_limit could split at 29.5, 30.1, 30.2...). Additionally, different feature orderings at different depths produce structurally different but equally valid trees with the same training accuracy. Without constraints like max_depth or min_samples_leaf, the space of possible trees is effectively infinite — making regularization essential to prevent overfitting.

08 — Supervised Learning

Regression
Analysis

(a) Linear Regression

Linear regression models the relationship between a continuous dependent variable and one or more independent variables by fitting a hyperplane that minimizes the sum of squared residuals. Expressed as ŷ = β₀ + β₁x₁ + ... + βₙxₙ, each coefficient βᵢ represents the expected change in the output per unit change in feature xᵢ, all else held constant. It assumes linearity, independence of errors, homoscedasticity, and approximate normality of residuals. Linear regression is prized for interpretability and is widely used for forecasting continuous quantities such as casualty counts, response times, or insurance costs.

(b) Logistic Regression

Logistic regression is a classification algorithm that models the probability of a binary or multi-class outcome by applying the logistic (sigmoid) function to a linear combination of input features. Despite its name, it is a discriminative probabilistic classifier, not a regression tool. The output is always a probability in (0, 1), thresholded to produce a class label. It is widely used in medicine, credit scoring, and risk classification because it outputs interpretable coefficient estimates as log-odds ratios and provides calibrated probability scores.

(c) Similarities and Differences

Both models fit a weighted linear combination of input features using optimization. However, linear regression outputs an unbounded continuous real value and minimizes mean squared error, while logistic regression outputs a class probability via the sigmoid function and optimizes binary cross-entropy loss through maximum likelihood estimation. Linear regression assumes Gaussian noise in the response; logistic regression assumes a Bernoulli outcome distribution. Linear regression is fundamentally inappropriate for classification tasks because its predictions can fall outside [0,1] and have no probabilistic meaning for class boundaries.

(d) The Sigmoid Function

Yes — logistic regression uses the sigmoid function to map any real-valued linear score to a probability in (0, 1). When the linear score is very large, the sigmoid approaches 1; when very negative, it approaches 0. The function is differentiable everywhere, enabling efficient gradient-based optimization. The S-curve shape creates a smooth, probability-calibrated decision boundary rather than a hard, arbitrary threshold.

σ(z) = 1 / (1 + e⁻ᶻ)   where   z = β₀ + β₁x₁ + ... + βₙxₙ

(e) Maximum Likelihood Estimation

Logistic regression is trained by finding the parameter vector β that maximizes the likelihood of observing the training labels given the input features — the Bernoulli log-likelihood: L(β) = Σ [yᵢ log σ(zᵢ) + (1−yᵢ) log(1−σ(zᵢ))]. Maximizing this equals minimizing binary cross-entropy loss. Unlike linear regression (which has a closed-form solution), logistic regression's MLE is solved iteratively. The MLE framework also provides principled confidence intervals and hypothesis tests on coefficients, making the model highly auditable for policy purposes.

09 — Final Findings

Conclusions &
Takeaways

01

Road Accidents Are Patterned, Not Random

After analyzing over 104,000 collision records, the most striking finding is that serious road accidents are far from unpredictable. The data reveals powerful, repeating patterns: collisions cluster in time during rush hours and late-night windows, concentrate geographically around urban centers and motorway junctions, and recur across the same combinations of speed, lighting, and road type. The notion that crashes are purely random events or solely the result of individual driver error is simply not supported by the evidence. Environment and infrastructure play starring roles — which means that improving design, lighting, and enforcement can translate directly into saved lives.

02

Rural Roads at Night Are the Deadliest Context

Across every analysis — association rules, clustering, decision trees, and regression — the same dangerous combination surfaced repeatedly: rural roads, at night, without adequate lighting, at high speeds. This profile accounts for a disproportionate share of fatal outcomes relative to its frequency in the dataset. Urban roads see more total accidents due to traffic volume, but rural fatal accidents cluster tightly around these contextual conditions. Investment in rural road lighting, speed enforcement on rural A-roads, and redesigned hotspot junctions identified through DBSCAN clustering could yield outsized reductions in fatalities at relatively modest infrastructure cost.

03

Machine Learning Can Meaningfully Predict Severity

The best-performing model — a Decision Tree at depth 7 using entropy splitting — achieved nearly 78% accuracy on a three-class severity classification task using only eight scene-level features. Logistic Regression reached an AUC of 0.791 on the binary serious/non-serious task. These results, achieved from features routinely recorded at any collision scene, suggest that real-time risk scoring is technically feasible. Emergency dispatch systems that flagged incoming calls as potentially fatal could prioritize faster response for the cases most likely to benefit from rapid intervention — a genuinely life-saving application of this data.

04

Friday Afternoon and Weekend Nights Are High-Risk Windows

Temporal analysis consistently highlighted Friday as the single most dangerous day for total collision count, with a secondary peak on Saturday nights. Morning and evening rush hours dominate weekday frequency, but severity shifts after dark — fewer total accidents but a significantly higher proportion of serious and fatal outcomes. Fatigue, possible impairment, and higher speeds after midnight compound to worsen outcomes. Public safety campaigns, targeted patrol deployment, and road safety messaging achieve the greatest cost-effectiveness when concentrated on these specific time windows rather than distributed uniformly across the entire week.

05

Data Science Is a Powerful Ally for Road Safety Policy

This project demonstrated that the complete data science lifecycle — from raw data cleaning through unsupervised discovery (PCA, Clustering, ARM) to supervised prediction (Naïve Bayes, Decision Trees, Logistic Regression) — can extract rich, actionable intelligence from road collision records. The insights translate directly into recommendations: where to install more street lighting, where to deploy speed cameras, when to staff more emergency units, and which road types to prioritize for redesign. Road safety is a societal challenge costing lives and billions annually. Data science, applied rigorously and communicated clearly, offers one of the most powerful and cost-effective tools we have to make our roads safer for every person who uses them.
